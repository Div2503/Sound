How our ears catch sound?







Outer ear :


  • The outer part of ear that is visible to us and looks like a funnel is called pinna.

  • It is attached to 2 to 3 centimetres long tube called ear canal or ear tube. The sound waves are collected by pinna and are sent in this tube.

  • At the end of ear canal is a thin, elastic and circular membrane called "eardrum" (also called tympanum) is tightly stretched.

  • When sounds reach the eardrum, it vibrates and transfers the sound into the middle part of ear.

  • Pinna, ear canal and eardrum are some of the major parts of the outer ear



Middle ear :


  • Middle ear is a cavity with three important bones, linked to one another, called the Malleus (Hammer shaped), Incus (Anvil shaped) and Stapes (Stirrup shaped).

  • One end of Hammer is attached to the ear drum and the oter end is attached to incus.

  • Other end of anvil is attached to the stapes and stapes's one end is connected to the oval window.

  • These three bones are responsile for the amplification of sound before passing them to the oval window.

  • Sound waves flow through them and reach the oval window through which they enter the inner part of the ear.



Inner ear :


  • The inner part of the ear has a coiled tube called Cochlea. This Cochlea is directly connected to the oval window.

  • Cochlea is filled with a liquid which contains nerve cells.

  • These nerve cells are sensitive to sound. They convert sound waves into signals readable by brain

  • The end of Cochlea is connected to the Auditory nerve to which the signals are carried by the nerve cells and transfers these to the Brain.
